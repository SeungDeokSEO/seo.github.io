🔹 **Beyond Economic and Political Motives: Exploring the Religious Dynamics in Gulf States’ Foreign Aid Allocation (with Paul Un)**

**Abstract**: Foreign aid scholarship has predominantly focused on the economic and political motivations of donors. However, the impact of social identities, particularly religious identity, on aid allocation has been comparatively unexplored. This study leverages social identity theory to examine how religious affinities between donors and recipients shape aid practices. We investigate the Gulf States' foreign aid allocation to Indonesia, a predominantly Muslim country with diverse religious demographics. Previous research has highlighted that Gulf countries such as Kuwait, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the United Arab Emirates tend to direct more aid to Muslim nations. Previous studies, however, have predominantly concentrated on Middle Eastern and North African countries and have relied extensively on cross-national comparisons. Such approaches fail to fully reveal the impact of religious identity on aid practices. Based on original government datasets from donors and recipients, our research demonstrates that Gulf countries direct more aid toward Indonesian regions with substantial Muslim populations and areas where members of Islamic political parties were elected in significant numbers in the 2019 legislative election. This subnational approach not only highlights the nuances of religious identity in aid distribution but also our understanding of non-traditional donor behaviors.


🔹 **Does Foreign Aid Create Disharmony among NGOs? The case of Palestine**

**Abstract**: Despite the recognized importance of inter-NGO cooperation, existing research has largely overlooked how such collaboration occurs at the level of aid-recipient countries. Utilizing an original dataset from the Palestinian NGO community, this study investigates the influence of foreign aid on inter-NGO cooperation. Based on a difference-in-means analysis, the findings reveal that NGOs receiving more donor aid demonstrate higher levels of inter-NGO cooperation. Meanwhile, NGOs perceiving unfairness in the aid distribution system are associated with reduced collaboration. Interviews with senior NGO representatives complement these quantitative results and help us capture the broader mechanism of how foreign aid affects inter-NGO cooperation.
